Day 73 - Hydraulic Calculation Basics — What Modelers Need to Know

1. Why This Matters
Hydraulic calculations determine whether water reaches the most remote sprinkler head at the required pressure and flow rate. As a modeler you do not perform these calculations — the fire engineer does. But your pipe routing decisions directly affect the calculation results. A re-routed pipe, a changed diameter, or an added fitting can invalidate a calculation the engineer has already completed.

2. Core Concept
What hydraulic calculations determine:
- The pressure required at the inlet (fire pump or town supply) to deliver design flow at the most remote head
- Whether pipe sizes are adequate for the required flow and friction losses
- The number of simultaneously operating heads (design area) that must be supplied

Key inputs that come from the BIM model:
- Pipe lengths (longer pipes = more friction loss = more pressure required)
- Pipe diameters (smaller diameter = more friction loss)
- Number of fittings (each elbow, tee, reducer adds equivalent pipe length)
- Elevation changes (height increases pressure demand)

What this means for modelers:
- Do not change pipe sizes without notifying the fire engineer
- Do not add excessive fittings (unnecessary bends, offsets) without flagging to the engineer
- Do not increase pipe length significantly by re-routing without notifying the engineer
- When re-routing is unavoidable, provide the new pipe lengths and fitting count to the engineer for recalculation
- In Revit: use the pipe length parameter and fitting count in schedules to track changes. A pipe that grows from 4 m to 12 m due to coordination re-routing is a reportable change.

Equivalent Length of Common Fittings (approximate, for reference only)
- 90° elbow: 1–3 m equivalent length depending on pipe size
- Tee (branch): 3–7 m equivalent length depending on pipe size

3. Real Project Lens
A coordination re-route moves a branch line around a large duct, adding 3 elbows and 6 m of extra pipe. The modeler flags this to the fire engineer with a note: "Branch line BL-04, Level 3: re-routed +6 m, +3 × 90° elbows due to mechanical duct conflict at grid C-5." The engineer recalculates and confirms the existing pipe size is still adequate, or requests an upsized pipe.

4. BIM Check
- Check: Any pipe re-route that adds more than 2 m of pipe length or more than 2 fittings has been flagged to the fire engineer
- Check: Pipe sizes have not been changed during coordination without engineering review
- Check: Elevation changes in the pipe routing are correctly modeled — no assumed level runs where there is a floor-to-floor change

5. Common Mistake
Re-routing fire protection pipes freely during coordination without notifying the engineer, then submitting the model as if the hydraulic calculation still applies. The calculation was done on the original routing — any change to the network may require recalculation.

6. Today's Action
Identify one branch line re-route on your current or sample project that involved adding pipe length or fittings. Write a brief coordination note to the fire engineer (2–3 sentences) describing the change: location, old length, new length, and reason for re-route.
