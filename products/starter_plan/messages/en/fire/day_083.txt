Day 83 - Pressure and Flow at the Most Remote Head — What Modelers Need to Know

1. Why This Matters
The most remote head in a fire protection system is the hardest point for water to reach — it has the longest pipe run, the most friction losses, and the greatest elevation change. The hydraulic calculation is designed around this head. As a modeler, you must understand this concept because any change to the routing between the supply and the most remote head directly affects system performance.

2. Core Concept
The Most Remote Head (Design Point):
- The fire engineer identifies the "design area" — typically the group of simultaneously operating heads in the worst-case location, usually the farthest cluster from the riser
- The hydraulic calculation confirms that the system delivers the required minimum pressure and flow at this design point
- Typical minimum operating pressure at a sprinkler head: 0.5–1.0 bar (50–100 kPa) depending on head type and standard
- Typical minimum flow per head: 57–115 L/min for pendant heads in standard hazard applications

What Increases Pressure Loss (and therefore affects the most remote head):
- Longer pipe runs (more friction loss)
- More fittings (each fitting has an equivalent pipe length)
- Smaller pipe diameter (much higher friction loss — varies with diameter to the 5th power)
- Higher elevation of the head relative to the pump (static pressure loss: ~0.1 bar per metre of height)

What Modelers Must Protect:
- The pipe route from the riser to the design area must not be lengthened without notification
- The pipe sizes on this critical path must not be reduced
- The number of fittings on this path must not be increased without notification
- If coordination forces a re-route of this critical path, flag it immediately — it is not the same as re-routing a branch serving one head

Identifying the Most Remote Head in the Model:
- Ask the fire engineer for the design area drawing or hydraulic calculation sketch
- In Revit, create a view filter that highlights the pipes on the critical path — this helps you know which pipes require special care during coordination

3. Real Project Lens
A coordination re-route moves the cross main around a large duct penetration, adding 4 m of pipe and 2 additional elbows on the critical path to the most remote head. The modeler flags this immediately: "Cross main re-route at Grid D-8, Level 2 — critical path to design area extended by 4 m and 2 × 90° elbows. Fire engineer review required." The engineer recalculates and requests an upsized cross main from DN100 to DN125 on this section.

4. BIM Check
- Check: The fire engineer's hydraulic calculation or design area sketch has been obtained and the critical path pipes are identified in the model
- Check: Any coordination change that lengthens pipe, adds fittings, or reduces pipe diameter on the critical path has been flagged to the fire engineer
- Check: Elevation changes of pipes on the critical path are accurately modeled — no assumed flat runs where a floor-to-floor drop exists

5. Common Mistake
Treating all fire protection pipes as equally flexible for coordination. A branch line serving one head can be re-routed with minimal hydraulic impact. The cross main on the critical path to the remote design area cannot be re-routed freely — every metre and fitting matters.

6. Today's Action
Ask your fire engineer (or review the hydraulic calculation on a sample project) to identify the most remote head / design area. Mark the critical path in your Revit model using a colour-coded pipe filter. Write a note on any coordination constraint on this path.
