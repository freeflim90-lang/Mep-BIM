Day 72 - Condensate Drain Piping — Slope and Connection

1. Why This Matters
Condensate drainage is one of the most overlooked HVAC coordination issues. A poorly modeled drain pan connection or missing slope causes pooling, overflow, and water damage — all avoidable with correct BIM coordination.

2. Core Concept
- Every FCU, AHU, and chilled beam produces condensate that must drain by gravity to the nearest sanitary stack
- The condensate drain pipe must slope continuously — minimum 1:50 — from the equipment outlet to the stack
- In Revit: connect the FCU drain connector to a sloped pipe routed to the plumbing stack
- Coordination check: does the drain route conflict with ductwork or cable tray below the ceiling void?
- Drain pan invert level must be at the correct height — not connected symbolically at the same elevation as the outlet

3. Real Project Lens
On a hotel project, 14 FCU condensate drains were modeled horizontally with no slope. The plumbing engineer flagged the error before the model was issued. Re-routing with correct slope added 120mm of vertical drop per connection — a small change that would have caused ceiling water damage if undetected.

4. BIM Check
- Check: the condensate drain pipe has a continuous downward slope from FCU outlet to the sanitary stack
- Check: no ductwork or cable tray blocks the drain pipe route below the FCU
- Check: the FCU Revit family has a drain connector at the correct height relative to the drain pan

5. Common Mistake
Beginners connect condensate drains at the same elevation as the FCU outlet, treating it as a flat pipe rather than a sloped gravity drain.

6. Today's Action
Sketch the condensate path for one FCU: start point (drain pan), slope direction, and discharge point to the plumbing stack.
