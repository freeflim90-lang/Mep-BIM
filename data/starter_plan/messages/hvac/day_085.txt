Day 85 - HVAC As-Built Requirements

1. Why This Matters
After installation, the as-built model must reflect what was actually built — not what was designed. Understanding what changes in the field and why prepares you for the as-built update process.

2. Core Concept
- Common as-built changes in HVAC: equipment relocated during installation, duct routes modified at site to avoid unforeseen clashes, access panels added or relocated, duct sizes changed due to late coordination decisions
- As-built accuracy levels vary: some EIRs require millimeter-accurate geometry; others require only system-level accuracy (correct connections and parameters)
- Most critical as-built data for facilities management: equipment location, connection sizes, valve locations, access panel locations
- Update process: site team marks up drawings during construction; BIM modeler updates the Revit model after each zone is completed, not as a batch at the end
- LOD 500: as-built models are sometimes described as LOD 500, meaning actual installed conditions are verified and recorded in the model

3. Real Project Lens
On a commercial project, the HVAC contractor made 12 routing changes during installation — all approved via RFI but never updated in the BIM model. When the facilities team received the as-built model, 3 fire damper access panels shown in the model were not at their physical installed locations. Ceiling tiles had to be removed in multiple locations to find the actual damper positions.

4. BIM Check
- Check: a process exists to record site changes from RFIs and site instructions and update the BIM model accordingly
- Check: all approved routing changes are incorporated in the Revit model before the as-built package is issued
- Check: access panel locations in the as-built model match their physically installed locations

5. Common Mistake
Beginners treat as-built updates as a batch activity at project end. Updating as changes are approved is far easier than reconstructing what changed after construction is complete.

6. Today's Action
Think of one HVAC change that typically happens on site (a duct re-routed around unexpected structure). Write down what information you would need from the site team to update the model accurately.
