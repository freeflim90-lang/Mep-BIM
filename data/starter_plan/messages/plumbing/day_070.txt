Day 70 - Week 2 Review — Slope Modeling Summary

1. Why This Matters
The first ten days of the plumbing deep-dive established the technical foundation every MEP BIM coordinator needs: how sanitary systems are classified, what slope rules apply, and how to model slope correctly in Revit. This review consolidates those lessons so they stay accessible when you need them on a real project.

Key modeling habits established this week:
- Every horizontal drainage pipe must have a slope value set
- System classification (Soil, Waste, Vent) must be applied to every pipe
- Invert levels must be checked at every branch junction

3. Real Project Lens
During a coordination review on a residential tower, the BIM manager ran a Revit filter to highlight all drainage pipes with Slope = 0. Over 40 pipes lit up — mostly short branches connecting WC pans to the soil stack. Each one had to be corrected before the coordination drawing could be issued. The fix took half a day. A weekly self-review habit would have caught those errors the same day they were modeled.

4. BIM Check
- Check: All horizontal drainage pipes in your model have a slope value greater than zero
- Check: System parameters (Soil, Waste, Vent) are assigned to every pipe segment — no unclassified pipes
- Check: Invert levels at branch junctions are documented and show a consistent downstream fall

5. Common Mistake
A common mistake is treating system classification as optional during the modeling phase and planning to add it later. In practice, "later" rarely happens before the coordination issue. Unclassified pipes cause incorrect schedule counts, clash filter gaps, and coordination issues that slow the whole team down.

6. Today's Action
Run a Revit schedule or filter on your plumbing model to check three things: (1) all horizontal drain pipes have Slope > 0, (2) all pipes have a System Classification assigned, and (3) no two adjacent pipe segments have identical invert levels. Document any gaps you find.
