Day 70 - Pump Room Layout — Arrangement, Discharge Headers, and Isolation

1. Why This Matters
The pump room is the heart of the piping system. Getting the equipment arrangement correct in BIM before construction prevents costly structural modifications and ensures maintenance access is preserved.

2. Core Concept
- Pump arrangement: in large systems, multiple pumps operate in parallel — each with isolation valves on suction and discharge, a non-return valve on discharge, and a pressure gauge
- Suction header: a large-diameter pipe that collects water from the chiller and distributes it to the pump inlets
- Discharge header: collects water from all pump outlets and delivers it to the distribution system
- Valve placement: isolation valves must be accessible — this drives pump spacing and aisle widths in the model
- Clearance: allow 600mm minimum between pump side and wall for valve access; 900mm in front of pump for motor removal

3. Real Project Lens
On a commercial project, the pump room layout was modeled with pumps 450mm from the wall — sufficient for pipe connections but not for motor removal during maintenance. Facilities engineers discovered this after occupation when the first pump motor needed replacement. A temporary wall panel had to be cut to allow extraction.

4. BIM Check
- Check: each pump has isolation valves on both suction and discharge sides that are reachable without climbing over pipework
- Check: non-return valves are modeled on the discharge side of each pump
- Check: there is a 900mm clear access corridor in front of each pump for motor and impeller removal

5. Common Mistake
Beginners model pump rooms to fit the equipment on plan without verifying that maintenance access clearances are met. Pump rooms that look clean on screen often fail in practice.

6. Today's Action
Sketch a simple pump room layout with two parallel pumps, suction header, discharge header, isolation valves, and a maintenance aisle. Check that 900mm front clearance exists for each pump.
