Day 81 - TAB — Testing, Adjusting, and Balancing — What BIM Supports

1. Why This Matters
TAB is the commissioning process that verifies HVAC systems deliver the correct airflow and water flow to every space. BIM models support TAB — but only if the model carries accurate system data.

2. Core Concept
- TAB (Testing, Adjusting, and Balancing) is performed after installation to verify airflow and water flow meet design specifications at every terminal
- The TAB engineer uses the BIM model's schedule data to understand the design airflow for each supply diffuser, VAV box, and FCU
- For BIM to support TAB: every terminal device must have the design airflow in its parameters; all equipment must have correct system assignment
- Volume control dampers must be modeled because TAB engineers adjust them during balancing — if they are missing from the model, the TAB report cannot reference them
- After TAB: some as-balanced airflows will differ from design — update BIM parameters to reflect actual balanced values if required by the EIR

3. Real Project Lens
On an office project, the TAB engineer requested the HVAC airflow schedule from the BIM model before the balancing run. The schedule showed 22 supply diffusers with no airflow value — the families were placed but the CFM parameter was never filled in. The TAB engineer had to use the engineer's paper design schedules instead, working around the BIM model entirely.

4. BIM Check
- Check: every supply and return diffuser in the model has a design airflow value (CFM or L/s) in its properties
- Check: volume control dampers are modeled at their installed locations and appear in the HVAC schedule export
- Check: the HVAC system schedule exported from Revit matches the engineer's design airflow schedule before the TAB run

5. Common Mistake
Beginners model ductwork geometry correctly but rarely fill in flow parameters. The model is geometrically useful but not usable for commissioning or facilities management.

6. Today's Action
Open any HVAC model and check 5 supply diffusers. How many have a design airflow value in their properties?
