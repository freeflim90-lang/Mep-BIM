Day 77 - Branch Pipe vs. Structure Clashes

1. Why This Matters
Branch lines that clash with structural beams, columns, or concrete slab require either penetration sleeves or pipe rerouting. Both solutions involve cost and coordination effort. Identifying structural clashes early — before concrete is poured or steelwork is erected — is far cheaper than cutting penetrations or redesigning pipe routes on site.

2. Core Concept
Branch pipe vs. structure clashes fall into three categories:

Category 1 — Pipe Passes Through a Structural Beam
- Steel beam: penetration is possible but must be structurally approved. A circular opening is cut through the web. Size, location, and spacing of penetrations are governed by structural engineering rules.
- Concrete beam: coring post-pour is expensive and weakens the beam. Penetrations should be pre-formed (cast-in sleeves) — modeled in BIM before concrete is poured.
- Rule: never assume beam penetration is acceptable — every penetration must be approved by the structural engineer.

Category 2 — Pipe Runs Alongside a Column or Wall
- A column or wall narrowing the ceiling zone may force a pipe to drop below its designed elevation
- Resolution: coordinate the routing elevation before the structural model is finalised

Category 3 — Pipe Penetrates a Concrete Slab (Floor-to-Floor Runs)
- Vertical pipes (risers, drops between floors) must pass through concrete slabs via pre-formed sleeves
- The sleeve must be large enough to allow for pipe movement (thermal expansion) and firestop material
- Minimum sleeve diameter: pipe OD + 50 mm for small pipes, larger for pipes with insulation

Sleeve Coordination in BIM
- Model every slab penetration sleeve as a generic family or structural opening request
- Tag each sleeve with: pipe diameter, system name, elevation, and firestop specification reference
- Issue structural opening requests to the structural engineer at the earliest model stage — before RC slab drawings are issued for construction

3. Real Project Lens
A fire protection riser passes through 8 concrete slabs in a 10-storey building. The BIM modeler creates 8 sleeve families at the correct slab locations and issues a Structural Opening Request drawing showing each sleeve with its required diameter and centerline coordinate. The structural engineer incorporates the sleeves into the slab reinforcement drawings. No post-pour coring is needed.

4. BIM Check
- Check: All branch line vs. structural beam clashes have been identified and flagged — no assumed penetrations without structural approval
- Check: All slab penetrations for fire protection pipes are modeled as sleeves with correct diameter and coordinates
- Check: Structural opening request documents have been issued to the structural engineer before slab drawings are finalised

5. Common Mistake
Routing a branch line through a structural beam web without notifying the structural engineer, assuming it is the same as cutting a hole in a partition wall. Structural beam penetrations require engineering assessment — the beam web has minimum edge distance requirements and a maximum penetration size limit.

6. Today's Action
Identify one structural beam that a fire protection branch line must cross on your current or sample project. Look up the beam size, then check whether a penetration through the web is geometrically possible given: pipe OD, minimum edge distance (typically beam depth / 4 from the top/bottom flange), and maximum hole size (typically beam depth / 3).
